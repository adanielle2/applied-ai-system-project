Three Core Techniques (Used by All Platforms)
🤝

Collaborative Filtering

"People who liked what you liked also liked…" — the classic approach. The system finds users with similar listening histories and recommends what those users enjoyed.

How it works: Decomposes a giant user-song interaction matrix into low-dimensional embedding vectors using matrix factorization. Similarity between vectors = likely interest.

Spotify's twist: Trains on 700M user playlists. Two songs that co-appear on many playlists are considered similar — no audio analysis needed.

Weakness: Fails for brand-new tracks (the "cold start" problem — no interaction data yet).

🎵

Content-Based Filtering

Analyzes the audio and metadata of songs themselves — no user history needed. Recommends tracks that are acoustically or lyrically similar to what you've liked.

Audio features used: Tempo (BPM), energy, danceability, valence (mood), loudness, key, acousticness, MFCCs (timbral texture).

Modern upgrade: Spotify and others now use large language models (LLMs) to embed lyrics and metadata into semantic vectors, capturing meaning beyond raw audio.

Strength: Works even for new songs with zero play history — solves the cold start problem.

🧠

Deep Learning & Transformers

The newest layer. Neural networks model the sequence of your listening — understanding not just what you like, but when and why, based on context.

Spotify: Uses RNNs and Transformer models to capture short-term "listening session" context. If you've been playing upbeat songs for 30 mins, it predicts your next track accordingly.

YouTube: Uses Transformer models to learn which past actions (like, skip, share) are most relevant to your current moment — e.g., workout vs. wind-down.

Strength: Understands intent and context, not just taste patterns.

Platform-by-Platform Breakdown
Spotify
The gold standard in music recommendation
  • Collaborative filtering on 700M user playlists
  • Matrix factorization for user/song embeddings
  • NLP on lyrics + blog posts via LLM semantic embeddings
  • RNN/Transformer session models for short-term context
  • Context-aware profiles (split by time-of-day, device, mood)
  • Discover Weekly, Daily Mix, Radio all use different sub-models
  • 1,800+ interaction types captured at 8M events/sec
YouTube Music
Two-stage funnel with Transformer ranking
  • Stage 1: Candidate generation narrows 100M+ songs to ~1,000
  • Stage 2: Transformer model ranks candidates by predicted value
  • Signals: watch history, skips, likes, session time, shares
  • Audio feature embeddings: BPM, key, energy, MFCCs
  • Context-aware: recognizes workout vs. chill listening patterns
  • Optimizes for "valued watch time" — not just clicks
  • Cross-platform signal: YouTube watch history informs music recs
Apple Music
Hybrid algo-torial: ML + human editorial
  • "Algo-torial" blend of human editors + machine learning
  • Strongest signals: library adds, hearts/favorites, Shazam history
  • Play completeness (finishing a song = positive signal)
  • Skip patterns carry significant weight
  • Deep Apple ecosystem integration (Siri, Watch, CarPlay data)
  • Human curators validate algorithmic playlists for quality
  • Recently expanded ML stack with on-device processing
Amazon Music
Voice-first, cross-platform behavioral signals
  • Leverages Alexa voice commands as a unique behavioral signal
  • Collaborative filtering + content-based hybrid approach
  • Cross-platform signals: shopping, Audible, Prime Video patterns
  • Personalized stations adapt to mood and activity
  • Similar multi-stage candidate generation + ranking pipeline
  • Amazon's broader recommendation engine powers music too (35% of sales attributed to recs)

⚠️ The Cold Start Problem — The Hardest Challenge in Music Recommendation

Collaborative filtering is powerful but breaks down completely for new releases: if a song has zero plays, it has no interaction data, and the model has nothing to learn from. Platforms solve this with multiple strategies:

  • Content-based bootstrapping: Analyze the audio of the new track and find similar-sounding songs that already have interaction data. Use the new track's audio embeddings to place it in the right neighborhood of the latent space.
  • Editorial seeding: Human curators (especially at Apple Music and Spotify) manually place new tracks into playlists, giving them initial exposure and generating the first interaction signals.
  • Artist-graph transfer: If a song is by an artist with existing history, the model leverages the artist's fan base to seed initial recommendations.
  • A/B exposure testing: Carefully show new tracks to a small % of users with similar tastes and rapidly learn from their reactions before wider rollout.
